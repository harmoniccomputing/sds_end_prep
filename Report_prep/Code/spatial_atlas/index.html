Cluster Detection

Voter Turnout is Geographically StructuredI = 0.614, p < 0.001

HYPOTHESIS / QUESTION: Does Tobler's First Law hold for democratic participation in India? Do nearby constituencies vote similarly, and if so, does this form contiguous geographic regions?
VERDICT: Yes, powerfully. 135 hot spots (South + NE) and 156 cold spots (Hindi heartland) account for 57% of all matched seats. Two contiguous high-turnout zones separated by one contiguous low-turnout belt. Policy can be spatially targeted.
Visualizations: Charts 90, 96, 37, 39, 85, 88, 47, 84
❌ Without Geography

Histogram: turnout is roughly normal, mean 65.8%. Srinagar (38.6%) and Dhubri (92.1%) are isolated data points. No adjacency info.

➔
✔ With Geography

LISA reveals two contiguous hot-spot regions + one cold-spot belt. Mobilization in one PC has spillover. Policy can be spatially targeted.

Women's Exclusion is Institutional, Not RegionalI = 0.113

HYPOTHESIS / QUESTION: Is women's electoral exclusion concentrated in 'conservative' regions (predicting I > 0.4), or dispersed uniformly (predicting I near 0)? Can we distinguish regional cultural barriers from institutional ones?
VERDICT: I = 0.113 (near-random). The exclusion is everywhere. 119 zero-women PCs span every region, state, terrain type. This rules out regional culture and demands systemic reform: party quotas, deposit waivers, the Women's Reservation Bill.
Visualizations: Charts 91, 97, 65, 66, 86, 89, 68, 67, 69, 70, 80, 54, 46, 73

➔
✔ With Geography

The Silence Map proves 119 zero-women PCs are scattered across EVERY region. I = 0.113 = near-random = institutional. No 'problem state' to target.

Spatial Outlier Identification

Participation Clusters 2.2x More Than CompetitionIturnout = 0.614 vs Imargin = 0.277

HYPOTHESIS / QUESTION: Are turnout and margin driven by the same spatial process? If both were driven by the same underlying factors (e.g., state political culture), their Moran's I values should be comparable.
VERDICT: Turnout is 2.2x more structured. Turnout LISA: 57% in clusters. Margin LISA: 24%. Turnout determinants (literacy, infrastructure) are spatially correlated. Margin determinants (candidate quality, caste) are not.
Visualizations: Charts 93, 99, 38, 41, 74
❌ Without Geography

Two histograms: turnout and margin both look like distributions. Cannot tell one is geographically structured.

➔
✔ With Geography

Turnout LISA: 57% clustered. Margin LISA: 24%. You can predict turnout from neighbours; you cannot predict competition.

A: No. The scatter shows weak correlation (r = 0.08) but hides the fact that turnout I = 0.614 while margin I = 0.277. Only the map reveals this spatial asymmetry.

The Delimitation Freeze and Fiscal Geography Compound the Democratic Deficit

HYPOTHESIS / QUESTION: Does the 1971-based seat allocation co-locate with low turnout, creating a double penalty? Do fiscal transfers reflect or correct the geographic imbalance?
VERDICT: The same northern belt with lowest turnout has highest population growth since 1971. UP, Bihar, Rajasthan are both behaviourally and structurally underrepresented.

The Geography of Women's Power

A Spatial Analysis of India's Representation Crisis: Why the Women's Reservation Bill, Delimitation, and the North-South Divide Cannot Be Understood Without Maps

Vishakha Agrawal | Lab for Spatial Informatics, IIIT Hyderabad | SDS Project

Act I: The 27-Year Promise That Failed in 48 Hours

On April 17, 2026, the Constitution (131st Amendment) Bill was voted down in the Lok Sabha: 298 in favour, 230 against, falling 54 votes short of the two-thirds majority required. With it collapsed the vehicle for implementing the Women's Reservation Bill (Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam), passed unanimously in September 2023 but linked to a delimitation exercise that has now stalled.

The opposition argued that women's reservation was the wrapper around a demographic power grab. The bill proposed expanding Lok Sabha from 543 to 850 seats and allocating them using the 2011 Census. States that controlled their populations (the South) would lose relative power to states that did not (the Hindi belt). P. Chidambaram called the amendment "mischievous and diabolical." Sonia Gandhi said "delimitation, not women's quota, is the real issue."

This analysis examines whether the opposition's claim is spatially defensible, whether the government's assurances match the bill's mechanism, and what this means for women's representation.

Act II: The Two Incompatible Scenarios

There are two sets of numbers circulating. They come from different formulas and produce different outcomes. Understanding which one the bill actually mandates is the central question.

Scenario A: What the Bill Says (Population-Proportional)

The Delimitation Bill (Section 4, Section 8) mandates allocation "on the basis of the latest census figures." Article 81 of the Constitution requires that "the ratio between [seats] and the population of the State is, so far as practicable, the same for all States." PRS India computed the projections using this formula. Result: Tamil Nadu goes from 39 to 48 (+23%), Kerala from 20 to 22 (+10%), while UP goes from 80 to 133 (+66%) and Bihar from 40 to 69 (+73%). The South's share drops from 24.1% to 20.8%, a loss of 3.3 percentage points.

Scenario B: What Shah Claimed in Parliament (Flat 50%)

Home Minister Amit Shah, speaking in the Lok Sabha on April 16-17, gave specific numbers: Tamil Nadu 39 to "around 59," Kerala 20 to 30, Karnataka 28 to 42, the South total from 129 to 195. Every one of these numbers equals current seats x 1.5 (within rounding). Under this formula, every state maintains its current share. The South stays at ~24%.

The mathematical incompatibility: Shah's numbers require house sizes of 990 (for TN = 59) to 1,087 (for Kerala = 30) to be population-proportional. The bill caps the house at 850. Shah's numbers and the bill's mechanism cannot both be correct. The Moran's I on the Shah-minus-Bill gap is +0.459 (very strong clustering): the gap itself is geographic, favouring the South by 20+ seats over what the bill would deliver.

When a political assurance and a legislative mechanism produce different spatial distributions of the same resource (seats), does the gap between them cluster geographically?

Act III: The Demographic Question

The core argument for population-proportional delimitation is democratic equality: "one person, one vote, one value." States with more people deserve more seats. But the counter-argument is that states were penalized with fewer relative seats precisely because they invested in family planning. Is population growth spatially clustered? And if so, does the allocation formula amplify or dampen that clustering?

Generalized form

Spatial finding

Population growth is moderately spatially clustered (I = +0.166). The proportional allocation formula amplifies this to strong clustering (I = +0.363), a 2.2x amplification. The formula does not just reflect demographic reality; it concentrates it. The bar chart debate asks "is population-based allocation fair?" The spatial analysis asks the deeper question: "does the formula create geographic blocs of winners and losers that did not exist in the input data at comparable strength?" It does.

Act IV: The Fiscal Paradox

Southern states contribute disproportionately to federal tax revenue while receiving less per rupee contributed. Under delimitation, the states that gain the most seats are also the states that contribute the least fiscally and receive the most in transfers. Is this coupling spatial?

Hypothesis / Question
When two georeferenced variables (seat gain and fiscal dependence) are positively correlated, does their coupling cluster geographically, forming belts of states that simultaneously gain political power and receive fiscal transfers?
Generalized form
For any two coupled resource flows (political representation gain vs. fiscal transfer receipt), test whether the coupling is spatially structured or randomly distributed.
FISCAL-POLITICAL COUPLING: Seat Gain (size) vs Tax Return Ratio (color) | Who gains seats AND receives fiscal transfers?
Spatial finding

The fiscal-political coupling clusters strongly. Tax return ratio has I = +0.395 and GDP per capita has I = +0.346. States gaining the most seats (UP, Bihar, MP, Rajasthan) form a contiguous belt that also receives the most per rupee contributed (Bihar: Rs 922 per Rs 100, UP: Rs 333). States gaining the fewest relative seats (TN, Kerala, Karnataka) form a contiguous belt that contributes the most (Maharashtra: Rs 7.7, Karnataka: Rs 13.9). A scatter plot shows this correlation; a map shows it is geographic, not random. The political power shift runs along the same geographic axis as the fiscal transfer flow.

Act V: Where Will Women's Reserved Seats Actually Go?

The Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam reserves one-third of Lok Sabha seats for women. After delimitation, this means approximately 270 reserved seats (under the bill's mechanism) or 268 (under Shah's). But the geographic distribution of these seats differs sharply between the two scenarios.

Hypothesis / Question
When a corrective policy (women's reservation) is allocated proportionally to a redistributed base (delimited seats), does the corrective policy's geographic footprint align with the geography of the problem it aims to solve (women's political exclusion)?
Generalized form
For any proportionally allocated corrective policy (affirmative action quotas, disability access mandates, scholarship distribution), test whether the geographic distribution matches the geography of need.

Under the bill's mechanism, the Central belt gets more reserved seats than the South (66 vs 56), even though the South has a lower women's election rate (8.5% vs 12.5%) and arguably greater need. The East, which already elects the most women proportionally (17.1%, driven by West Bengal and Odisha), gets 59 reserved seats. The reservation formula allocates by total seats, not by women's exclusion. The North, where women barely contest (6.8% elected), gets only 23. A bar chart of "reserved seats by state" shows numbers. The map shows that the geographic distribution of the corrective policy does not match the geography of the problem.

Act VI: What This Analysis Would Look Like Without Maps

Every finding in this document was produced by Moran's I (a single statistic) and LISA cluster maps (a single visualization type). Without spatial methods, arriving at the same conclusions would require:

✗ Without spatial methods

The two-scenario discrepancy: Two bar charts of state-wise seats (Shah vs Bill), manual comparison of 30 state-pairs, then geographic domain knowledge to notice that the discrepancies cluster in the South. Three charts, manual inspection.

The formula amplification: Bar chart of population growth, separate bar chart of seat gains, manual inspection to notice that growth is scattered but gains are belted. Two charts plus geographic expertise.

The fiscal coupling: Scatter plot of gains vs tax ratio, separate table of which states gain, manual geographic overlay. Two charts plus a hand-drawn map.

The reservation mismatch: Bar chart of reserved seats, separate bar chart of current women elected, side-by-side comparison requiring geographic knowledge. Three charts.

Total: 10+ separate charts, manual overlay, geographic domain expertise, substantial effort to connect the dots across charts.

⇒
✓ With spatial methods

Two-scenario discrepancy: Moran's I on Shah-minus-Bill gap (I = +0.459). One number tells the analyst the gap is geographic. One LISA map shows where.

Formula amplification: Compare I on growth (+0.166) vs I on seat gain (+0.363). The ratio (2.2x) is the finding. Two numbers.

Fiscal coupling: Overlay both variables on one map. Both cluster on the same axis. One map.

Reservation mismatch: Map reserved seats against current success rate. Mismatch is immediately visible. One map.

Total: 4 maps, 6 Moran's I values. No geographic expertise required. The statistics detect patterns automatically and the maps show where they are.

The value of spatialization is not aesthetic. It is epistemic. Moran's I = +0.476 on the share change tells an analyst, without any knowledge of Indian geography, that the proposed reform has a spatial axis of inequality. A LISA map then shows exactly where that axis runs. The aspatial approach requires the analyst to already know the geography and manually detect patterns across multiple separate visualizations. The spatial approach automates detection, quantifies it, and localizes it, all in a single framework.

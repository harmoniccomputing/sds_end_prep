Act I: The 27-Year Promise That Failed in 48 Hours
The Core Question

When a resource (parliamentary seats) is redistributed using a formula, do the winners and losers form contiguous geographic belts? And if they do, is the reform fair?

What Happened

The 131st Amendment Bill was voted down on April 17, 2026. 298 for, 230 against, 54 short of two-thirds. The Women's Reservation Bill, passed unanimously in 2023, remains unimplemented.

The opposition argued that women's reservation was the wrapper around a demographic power grab. The bill proposed expanding Lok Sabha from 543 to 850 seats using the 2011 Census. States that controlled their populations (the South) would lose relative power to states that did not (the Hindi belt).

P. Chidambaram called the amendment "mischievous and diabolical." Sonia Gandhi said "delimitation, not women's quota, is the real issue."

This analysis uses Moran's I and LISA cluster maps to test whether the opposition's claim is spatially defensible.

Act III: The Spatial Evidence
Moran's I: the decision criterion for spatialization
Generalized Hypothesis

Given multiple georeferenced variables, which ones exhibit spatial autocorrelation (geographic clustering) and which are spatially random? Moran's I quantifies this automatically, without geographic expertise.

Key Finding: Formula Amplification

Population growth is moderately clustered (I=+0.17). The proportional allocation formula amplifies this to strong clustering (I=+0.36). Amplification factor: 2.2x. The formula does not just reflect demographics; it concentrates them.

Moran Case 1: Population Growth
I = +0.166 MODERATE | The demographic input
Generalized Hypothesis

Is population growth 1971-2011 spatially clustered? If it is, the allocation formula's input already has geographic structure. If not, the formula creates the structure.

This Dataset

Kerala grew 57%. Rajasthan grew 167%. Delhi grew 310%. After correcting for state reorganizations (Telangana from AP, Jharkhand from Bihar, Chhattisgarh from MP, Uttarakhand from UP), the spatial signal is moderate: I = +0.166.

Spatial Finding

Population growth is MODERATELY clustered (I=+0.166, not statistically significant at p<0.05). High-growth states do not all neighbor each other. But when this weak signal enters the proportional allocation formula, it emerges as STRONG clustering (I=+0.363). The formula amplifies 2.2x.

Moran Case 2: Seat Gain % (Bill)
I = +0.363 STRONG | The formula's output
Generalized Hypothesis

When a formula transforms a weakly clustered input into a resource allocation, does the output cluster more strongly? If so, the formula creates geographic inequality that did not exist at comparable strength in the input.

This Dataset

The proportional formula (seats = state pop / national pop x 815) converts population into seats. Rajasthan gains 84%, Bihar 73%, UP 66%, while Kerala gains 10% and TN 23%. The gains cluster in a Central-Western belt.

Spatial Finding

Seat gain % clusters strongly (I=+0.363), more than double the input clustering (I=+0.166). The formula amplifies weak demographic spatial structure by 2.2x. The Central belt (UP, MP, Rajasthan, Bihar) forms a contiguous corridor of above-average gains.

Moran Case 3: Share Change (Bill)
I = +0.476 VERY STRONG | The strongest signal
Generalized Hypothesis

When relative shares (not absolute gains) are computed, does the zero-sum nature of share redistribution amplify spatial clustering even further?

This Dataset

UP's share rises from 14.7% to 16.4% (+1.63pp). TN's share drops from 7.2% to 5.9% (-1.28pp). Kerala drops from 3.7% to 2.7% (-0.97pp). The share change is the STRONGEST spatial signal in the dataset at I = +0.476.

Spatial Finding

Share change is zero-sum: every percentage point gained by one state is lost by another. This zero-sum arithmetic concentrates gains and losses into contiguous belts. The red belt (Central: gaining share) and the blue belt (South: losing share) are not just correlated; they are geographic formations.

The Moran Scatter Plot
Slope = Moran's I = 0.614 (voter turnout)
Generalized Hypothesis

For any georeferenced variable, the Moran scatter plot (standardized value z vs spatial lag Wz) provides a complete spatial diagnostic. The slope equals Moran's I. The quadrants classify each unit as HH, HL, LH, or LL.

This Dataset

Voter turnout in Lok Sabha 2024 has slope = +0.614 (the strongest spatial signal). Points in the upper-right (HH: high turnout surrounded by high turnout) and lower-left (LL: low surrounded by low) dominate. The spatial structure is statistically significant.

Spatial Finding

No geographic knowledge is needed. The slope alone tells the analyst that turnout clusters geographically. The quadrants classify WHERE: HH = South and East, LL = Hindi belt urban areas. This is Anselin's (1995) standard diagnostic.

X-axis: Standardized value (z = (x - mean) / std)

Y-axis: Spatial lag (Wz = weighted mean of neighbors)

Slope of regression line = Moran's I

HH (hot spot) LL (cold spot) HL (outlier) LH (outlier)
Moran's I Eruption: 3D Spatial Statistics
Each bar = one constituency's contribution to global I
Generalized Hypothesis

Can local contributions to a global spatial statistic be decomposed and visualized as a 3D landscape, making abstract statistics physically tangible?

This Dataset

Each bar represents one constituency. Height = local Moran's I contribution. Color = LISA quadrant (red HH, blue LL). The 'eruption' shows which constituencies drive the global statistic.

Spatial Finding

The tallest bars (strongest contributors to spatial autocorrelation) cluster together, forming 'mountain ranges' of spatial structure. The Hindi belt LL cluster and the South-East HH cluster are visible as distinct geographic formations. Rotate and zoom to explore.

This is the spatial statistics analogue of a contribution plot in PCA. It decomposes global Moran's I into per-unit local contributions, revealing which spatial units are driving the overall pattern.

A flat landscape = no spatial autocorrelation. Tall clustered bars = strong geographic structure. Isolated tall bars = spatial outliers.

Spatial Finding

The histogram's conclusion is correct but incomplete. The LISA map adds WHERE. This changes the policy prescription from "raise national turnout" to "target the Hindi belt low-turnout corridor." The spatial method changes the conclusion.

Query 1b: Boxplot vs Choropleth
Statistical summary vs geographic distribution

This Dataset

The boxplot is the most common statistical summary in policy reports. It works well for non-spatial data. For georeferenced data with I > 0.25, it systematically misses the geographic structure that drives the variation.

Act V: Women's Political Geography
Candidature I = +0.30 (clusters) | Success I = +0.04 (random)
Generalized Hypothesis

Given two georeferenced variables measuring different stages of the same pipeline, can parallel LISA maps reveal which stage exhibits spatial structure and which does not?

This Dataset

Women's candidature rate clusters spatially (I=+0.30): Chhattisgarh, Maharashtra, MP, Gujarat form an HH belt. Bihar, Jharkhand, WB form an LL belt. But women's election success is spatially random (I=+0.04).

Spatial Finding

Geography drives who RUNS but not who WINS. The election process erases spatial structure. Parties nominate women in geographic belts (possibly due to regional party culture), but voter behavior does not follow the same spatial pattern. The pipeline has a spatial filter.

The scatter plot of candidature vs success shows "weak correlation" (r = +0.08). The LISA maps show the deeper truth: spatial structure exists in one but not the other. This distinction is invisible in any aspatial visualization.

The Silence Map
Constituencies with zero or one woman candidate
Generalized Hypothesis

Does the geography of ABSENCE (zero values of a participation variable) cluster spatially? If absence clusters, the barriers causing it are structural and geographic, not individual.

This Dataset

In 2024, dozens of Lok Sabha constituencies had zero or one woman candidate. These "silent" constituencies are not randomly scattered. They cluster in specific regions, revealing structural barriers aligned with geography.

Spatial Finding

The silence has a geography. The 33% reservation aims to break this silence. But under population-proportional delimitation, reserved seats go where total seats go (Central belt), not where silence is deepest (parts of North, East). The corrective policy's geography does not match the problem's geography.

This map inverts the usual analysis: instead of mapping where women ARE, it maps where women are NOT. The clustering of absence is as informative as the clustering of presence.

From candidature (I=+0.30) to success (I=+0.04): spatial filter

This Dataset

Women's candidature rate: I = +0.30 (moderate clustering). Women's election success: I = +0.04 (spatially random). The pipeline from running to winning erases geographic structure. The gauntlet is not geographic; it is political.

Policy implication: Reservation (33%) addresses the candidature stage by guaranteeing women's presence. But since election success is already spatially random, the reservation's geographic distribution (driven by delimitation) determines WHERE women enter Parliament, not WHETHER they win.

Act VI: 53 Years Frozen
Seats frozen since 1971 | Population grew unevenly
Generalized Hypothesis

When a resource allocation is frozen for decades while the underlying variable (population) changes unevenly across geography, does the resulting mismatch cluster spatially?

This Dataset

The 42nd Amendment (1976) froze Lok Sabha seats at the 1971 Census. The 84th Amendment (2001) extended the freeze to 2026. In 53 years, UP grew from 8.8 crore to 20 crore while Kerala grew from 2.1 to 3.3 crore. The mismatch is geographic.

Spatial Finding

The voters-per-seat distortion clusters strongly. UP constituencies average 25 lakh voters; Kerala constituencies average 17 lakh. The distortion is not random: it follows the demographic transition belt. States that completed the transition early (South) are over-represented per capita.

The freeze was designed to incentivize family planning. It succeeded: TFR dropped from 5.2 (1971) to 2.0 (2021). But it created a geographic equity problem: states that responded fastest to the incentive now face diluted representation.

The delimitation bill would correct this, but the correction itself creates geographic winners and losers (I = +0.476 on share change).

Act VII: The Fiscal Paradox
Tax return ratio I = +0.395 STRONG
Generalized Hypothesis

When fiscal transfers between subnational units are georeferenced, do net contributors and net recipients form contiguous geographic belts rather than being randomly distributed?

This Dataset

For every Rs 100 contributed in tax, Bihar receives Rs 922 back. UP receives Rs 333. Maharashtra receives Rs 7.7. Karnataka receives Rs 13.9. The fiscal transfer ratio clusters strongly (I = +0.395).

Spatial Finding

Net fiscal contributors (Maharashtra, Karnataka, TN, Gujarat) form one contiguous belt. Net recipients (Bihar, UP, MP, Jharkhand) form another. The fiscal axis and the delimitation axis are the same geographic axis. States gaining seats are the same states receiving fiscal transfers.

The South's argument: "We controlled our populations, we contribute more fiscally, and now we lose political representation." The data supports this: all three variables (population control, fiscal contribution, seat share loss) cluster on the same geographic axis.

The "Punishment" for Population Control

Generalized Hypothesis

When a policy incentive (family planning) is linked to a resource (seats), and the incentive succeeds unevenly across geography, does the resulting "punishment" for compliance cluster spatially?

This Dataset

This is the moral core of the delimitation debate. The 1976 freeze was a promise: "control your population and your representation will be protected." The 2026 bill breaks that promise for exactly the states that kept it. The 3D surface shows the shape of this broken promise.

Party Alliances and Women's Candidature
Do party alliance patterns align with women's spatial geography?
Generalized Hypothesis

When a political variable (party alliance) and a social variable (women's candidature) are both georeferenced, do their spatial patterns align, suggesting that party culture drives gender outcomes?

This Dataset

NDA-dominated regions and women's candidature patterns are compared through LISA analysis. The question: do parties in certain geographic belts systematically nominate more or fewer women, and does this explain the spatial clustering of candidature?

Spatial Finding

Party alliance patterns have their own spatial structure. When overlaid with women's candidature LISA, the alignment is partial: some party-dominated belts correspond to high women's candidature, others do not. The spatial structure of women's representation is not fully explained by party geography.

This visualization tests whether party culture or regional culture drives women's candidature. If party culture dominates, the LISA clusters would align with party strongholds. If regional culture dominates, the clusters would cross party boundaries.

The Cost of Contesting: Deposit Forfeiture
Women who lose their deposit: a geographic pattern?
Generalized Hypothesis

When candidates fail to secure a minimum vote share (forfeit their deposit), does the geography of forfeiture cluster spatially, and does it differ between men and women candidates?

This Dataset

In India, candidates forfeiting deposits (less than 1/6 of votes) lose their Rs 25,000. Women candidates forfeit at higher rates in certain regions. This chart examines whether the financial punishment of contesting clusters geographically.

Spatial Finding

Deposit forfeiture patterns reveal the "viability floor" for women's candidature. In regions where women routinely forfeit, parties have no incentive to nominate them. This creates a self-reinforcing geographic trap: low viability leads to low nomination leads to low viability. The spatial structure of forfeiture predicts the spatial structure of silence.

The reservation bill would break this cycle by guaranteeing women's candidature in reserved seats. But the geographic distribution of reserved seats (driven by delimitation) determines where this cycle-breaking occurs. If reserved seats cluster in already-viable regions, the cycle persists elsewhere.

Act IX: Spatial vs Non-Spatial Clustering
k-Means ignores geography. LISA respects it.
Generalized Hypothesis

When a standard clustering algorithm (k-Means) and a spatial clustering method (LISA) are applied to the same georeferenced data, do they produce different groupings? Which is more policy-relevant?

This Dataset

k-Means clusters constituencies by electoral variables (turnout, margin, women's candidature) without considering geography. LISA clusters them by spatial autocorrelation. The k-Means clusters are scattered across the map; the LISA clusters form contiguous belts.

Spatial Finding

k-Means groups constituencies with similar VALUES regardless of location. LISA groups constituencies with similar values AND adjacent locations. For policy (which requires contiguous administrative zones), LISA clusters are actionable. k-Means clusters are not, because a policy cannot target scattered constituencies without geographic coherence.

This is the key methodological argument: aspatial clustering creates groups that cannot be administered. Spatial clustering creates groups that can. The choice of method determines whether the analysis is academically interesting or policy-relevant.

Dual Map: Turnout vs Victory Margin
Two variables, one geography. Do the patterns align?
Generalized Hypothesis

When two georeferenced variables are displayed on synchronized side-by-side maps, can visual comparison of their spatial patterns reveal correlations that scatter plots quantify but cannot localize?

This Dataset

Left map: voter turnout by constituency. Right map: victory margin by constituency. Where turnout is low (Hindi belt), are margins also different? The dual map lets the viewer compare spatial patterns directly, constituency by constituency.

Spatial Finding

The dual map reveals spatial co-occurrence that the scatter plot flattens. Regions of low turnout AND high margin (safe seats with disengaged voters) cluster in specific geographic areas. Regions of high turnout AND low margin (competitive, engaged) cluster in others. The co-occurrence IS the geographic finding.

A scatter plot of turnout vs margin shows a cloud of points with moderate correlation. The dual map shows that the correlation has a geographic address. This is the difference between "these variables correlate" and "these variables correlate HERE."

Constituency Aurora
Every constituency as a point of light in multi-dimensional space
Generalized Hypothesis

Can dimensionality reduction (projecting multi-variable constituency data into 2D/3D) reveal natural groupings that correspond to geographic regions, even when geography is not an input variable?

This Dataset

Each constituency plotted in a reduced-dimension space based on its electoral characteristics (turnout, margin, women's candidature, etc.). Color encodes region. If geographic regions emerge as clusters WITHOUT geography being an input, the electoral variables have a spatial axis.

Spatial Finding

Regional colors cluster in the aurora. The South (pink/red) separates from the Hindi belt (orange/yellow) in the reduced space. This means electoral characteristics alone, without any geographic input, are sufficient to predict region. Geography is embedded in the data.

This is the strongest possible argument for spatialization: even when you remove geography, it comes back. Electoral variables carry geographic information. If geography is already in the data, spatial analysis is not optional; it is necessary for valid inference.

Act X: Why Maps Beat Bar Charts
10+ aspatial charts vs 4 LISA maps + 6 Moran's I values
The Epistemic Argument

Spatial analysis does not just add maps. It automates geographic pattern detection, quantifies it with a single statistic (Moran's I), and localizes it (LISA). The value is epistemic, not aesthetic.

What This Analysis Proved

Every finding in this presentation (formula amplification, Bill-Shah incompatibility, fiscal coupling, reservation mismatch, punishment for population control) was detected by Moran's I and localized by LISA. None were visible in bar charts alone.

Conclusions
Five findings from spatial analysis, all invisible without maps. Presented April 22, 2026, five days after the 131st Amendment Bill failed.
1
Bill vs Shah: mathematically incompatible
Shah's per-state numbers = current x 1.5 exactly. The bill mandates population-proportional (Article 81). The gap clusters geographically at I = +0.459. The South is promised ~20 more seats than the bill would deliver.
2
The allocation formula amplifies spatial structure 2.2x
Population growth is moderately clustered (I = +0.17). The proportional seat formula amplifies this to strong clustering (I = +0.36). The formula creates geographic belts that did not exist at comparable strength in the input data.
3
Fiscal-political coupling runs along the same geographic axis
Tax return ratio I = +0.395. States gaining seats AND receiving fiscal transfers form a contiguous belt from Bihar through UP, MP, to Rajasthan. The South contributes fiscally and loses political weight simultaneously.
4
Women's reservation does not match the geography of exclusion
Central gets 66 reserved seats (most) despite 12.5% women already elected. The North, with 6.8% women elected (highest need), gets only 23. The formula allocates by total seats, not by exclusion.
5
None of these findings are visible without spatial analysis
Moran's I and LISA maps replace 10+ bar charts and manual geographic expertise with 4 maps and 6 statistics. The method is generalizable to any georeferenced resource allocation debate.
